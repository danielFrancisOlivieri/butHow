How this Page Gets to You

From Your Click

When you clicked on the link to this page, the slight press of your finger on the your mouse set in motion a journey all the way to Ashburn, Virginia and back. Specifically, to an office building on 1506 Moran Rd. You can see the destination right here . Your request was spliced up into different data packets. Each one of these packets took a different route to California, hoping from one computer to the next for hundreds of miles. When your request arrived at its destination, a server selected this exact page as the one you had ordered. Then, the server cut up this page into little bits and sent it back the way it came. Hundreds of computers likely participated in the effort to search for, find, and retrieve the words you are reading now. Radio waves ferried your data from computer to computer at a rate of 300,000 km per second. And all of this happened in a second or two. The internet has been designed exactly so that we do not notice the absurdly complex mechanisms that make it possible. The radio waves, the {number of computers}, the incomprehensible information stashed away in a far away place, these are all hidden from us. This is because the internet is designed to be used, not to be marvelled at. The only time the internet draws attention to itself is when it fails. When the internet is working it appears to be at our command, but when it fails we realize that we are actually at its mercy. There are 404 errors and spotty wifi connections. It’s in moments like these that the internet seems to have no mercy. Unlike other technology to rely on, we cannot see the internet. There is not a flat tire to replace, a nail to hammer in, or a cog to grease. There is not even a physical object to touch. In his interview with Bill Moyers, Joseph Campbell said, “Computers are like old testaments God, lots of rules and no mercy.” This metaphor hold especially true for the internet. It is everywhere yet cannot be seen anywhere. It is capable of miracles, but also capable of unexplained cruelty. Much like God, it ostensibly connects us by in practice only seems to divide us into our separate camps. This website is here to undeify the internet. We are going on this journey together. From Your Click

The process of events begins who you click on a link or hit enter on a search. When you click on a link that says google.com your computer sends that message to its wireless adapter. This chip inside of the computer takes the string of characters “google.com” and translates it into radio waves. But how does English to radio wave translation work? First, the url is broken down into binary. Radio waves are waves like those at the beach, they go up and they go down. Radios send signals by changing how fast these waves go. You start at the peak of one wave and then record how long it takes for the next one to come. One wave might take a second to go from one peak to the next. Another wave might take have a second to go from peak to peak. The speed of a wave is its frequency. Higher frequency means it’s capable of transferring more data. By assigning different characters to different frequencies, we can turn radio frequencies into a language. The brilliance of this technique is that the radio waves work as both the messenger and the message. This language of radio waves scurries around as constantly, invisibly and illegible to us humans.

Routers

Fortunately, there are routers that are literate in the language of radio waves. Routers are considered computers, but are nothing like a laptop or desktop. They have no screen and often have several antennae poking out of them. These routers are the traffic intersections of the internet. It is by establishing a radio signal connection with a router that a computer can connect to the internet at large. The job of the routers is to make sure information goes where it is intended to go. They are like the train stations of the internet. Once the router receives the radio waves, it has the name “google.com” but this isn’t enough. It needs to have the address of the website. The addresses of website are known as IP addresses. For example, the IP address of Google’s homepage is 172.217.6.174. Copy that and put it into your url search bar. It will take you there. Unfortunately, the name 172.217.6.174 is not easy for people to remember. This is where the DNS comes in. A Domain Name Server or DNS is the yellow pages of the internet. It’s job is to match every url to its IP address. When it gets a signal from the router, it starts to search for matching IP address. If the DNS can find the correct IP address, it will send that back. If it cannot find the right IP address, which is most of the time, the DNS will ask another DNS for help. If even then it can’t find the IP address, it will send back an error message. The IP address works like a building’s address. It informs the router where the web page is stored. Equipped with the IP address, the router’s next job is to ask the server at the specified IP address for the web page. The message asking for a web page is known as an HTTP GET request. A request looks like this:

GET / HTTP/1.1 Host: erbosoft .com Connection: keep-alive Cache-Control: max-age=0 Accept: text/html,application/xhtml+xml,application/xml;q=0.9,image/webp,*/*;q=0.8 User-Agent: Mozilla/5.0 (X11; Linux x86_64) AppleWebKit/537.36 (KHTML, like Gecko) Chrome/38.0.2125.104 Safari/537.36 Accept-Encoding: gzip,deflate,sdch Accept-Language: en-US,en;q=0.8 If-Modified-Since: Tue, 07 Feb 2012 04:44:06 GMT

Before it sends the message, though, the router packs the message up into one or more data packets. Each data packet is a maximum 1500 bytes. Each byte contains one character. Because HTTP GET requests are so small, they usually fit into just one data packet. Now that the HTTP GET request is in a package, the router can send it on the way to the server.

Travelling in Style

There are three fashionable ways for data packets to travel: copper, optic cables, or satellites. But these signals don’t go straight to their destination. They couch crash on different routers along. They can use upwards of 15 routers for a single data packet. For reference, just one Google search uses about 1000 computers in its 3000 mile circuit to get your information and get back. The internet has traffic jams in the same way that high ways do. This is not much of a problem, because data packets will simply avoid jams when they see them. This both makes the data packet get to its destination quicker and does not exacerbate the problem of the congested area.
