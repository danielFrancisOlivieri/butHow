The Google logo likes to play dress-up. On one day the letters are made out of portholes to celebrate Jules Verne, on another they’re forming the walls of a PAC-MAN game that you can actually play. Whatever the Google logo happens to be wearing, you can look under it to see the search bar. This search bar is one of the most powerful rectangles humanity has ever invented, along with credit cards and hundred dollar bills. Through this little slat of an input bar you can venture out to explore any province of the internet. Underneath this rectangle are two stouter rectangles labelled “Google Search” and “I’m Feeling Lucky.” Outside of the logo and these three rectangles, the page is white. Just plain white. There is a slice of grey at the bottom of the page, but that’s it. Google’s empire all began with its ability to make the internet simple and it has stuck to its guns.

This simplicity, however, requires vast complexity. You can pull back the curtain and see this complexity yourself. If you right click on Google’s homepage and select “View Page Source” you’ll get a new tab. Backstage you’ll see that the simple Google homepage is held in place by an entire novella of code (42,707 words to be exact). There are brackets, hashtag signs and long strands of letters that don’t even merit an attempt at pronunciation. To most people, myself included, this looks to be somewhere between gibberish and gobbledygook. Paradoxically, it’s this stuff that seems like nonsense that functions with the utmost order and precision.

Computer programs in general and webpages in particular have been designed so that we do not notice the absurdly complex mechanisms that make them possible. Just as the engine of a car is hidden underneath a hood, the thousands of lines of code are hidden behind the buttons we click and the graphics we stare at. This is because computers are designed to be used, not to be marvelled at. This abstraction, however, ends up giving computer code an air of sorcery. This leaves most people who use computers entirely in the dark. It leaves us unable to begin to understand the devices that so much of our lives depend on.

The fun of coding is that you get to invent the shit out of things with your keyboard. You can wizard things into existance with just your time and your intelligence. There is wittiness in coding. You can do things faster if you're smarter. No matter how good and fast a computer is, it's the genius of the algorithms in it that determines how quick it'll run. There is the potential for turning your language not just into words but into actual seeable things. You words are spells, they turn gears and crank cranks. There is a strength to it, a huge amount of respect.

It's also aesthetically beautiful, I think.

There is a thrill to seeing the underside of anything. You need to familiarize yourself with it. You need to get comfortable with the grossness of it, comfortable with the terror. This is the power of code. It's the power to split open the mind and let it actually wrap around the computer and control it. There is a lot of frustration. There will be a million nights of thinking. If only I saw that one parenthesis! There's a lot of testing. It is a meticulous process if done correctly. There are so many different mistakes to be made.
